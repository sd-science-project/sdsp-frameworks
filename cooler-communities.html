Unit Overview

What Students Investigate

Students embark on a journey of discovery, innovation, and action to understand global change in their communities. They learn how Earth systems interact and use systems thinking to model their understanding of global change using the Understanding Global Change (UGC) framework.

They collect data on land surface temperatures and vegetation in their communities, research how humans impact land surface temperatures, and as climate champions, design community maps that cultivate a cooler community for the future.

Using their evidence, students end by designing and presenting a data-backed solution for a campus "hot zone," such as adding shade, changing surfaces, or increasing vegetation, to help create a cooler community.

Anchoring Phenomenon
The surface temperature of the land differs across parts of our city.
Students observe San Diego County Land Surface Temperature (LST) data to determine causes and effects of varying temperatures across their city. Note: This anchoring phenomenon can be adapted to reflect the county or city where your students reside to maximize local relevance.
Unit Driving Question
"Why do land surface temperatures differ across different parts of our city?"

Unit Storyline

Lesson-by-Lesson Overview

Each lesson builds toward students' final model and written explanation of the unit driving question. Students use a Science Notebook throughout the unit and contribute to a Class Learning Tracker at the end of each lesson to record what they figured out and how it connects to the driving question. Click each lesson to expand.

  • 1
    What do we notice about temperatures in our city?
    70 min

    Students observe San Diego County Land Surface Temperature (LST) data and make initial observations and questions about why temperatures differ across the city. Students begin their UGC models and science notebooks.

    Investigation
  • 2
    How does sunlight interact with different surfaces?
    70 min

    Students investigate how sunlight can be absorbed or reflected by different land surfaces and how this affects temperature. They connect albedo to the LST patterns observed in Lesson 1.

    Investigation
  • 3
    How do plants affect land surface temperature?
    70 min

    Students explore how vegetation reduces land surface temperatures through shading and photosynthesis. They collect data on vegetation and LST in their local community.

    Investigation
  • 4
    How does urban development change temperatures?
    70 min

    Students investigate how building cities alters how much sunlight is absorbed or reflected and reduces space for plants to grow, connecting urban development to rising land surface temperatures.

    Discussion
  • 5–6
    How do human activities contribute to temperature change?
    70 min ×2

    Students research how burning oil, gas, and coal releases greenhouse gases that trap heat and raise air and land surface temperatures through the greenhouse effect.

    Modeling
  • 7–8
    How do air temperature and land surface temperature interact?
    70 min ×2

    Students build on their models to understand the feedback loop between air temperature and land surface temperature, and how heatwaves affect human health, especially in urban communities.

    Modeling
  • 9–10
    What patterns do we see in our community's temperature data?
    70 min ×2

    Students analyze and interpret LST and vegetation data from their own community, identifying hot zones and connecting local patterns to the causes they have studied.

    Investigation
  • 11
    What solutions could cool our community?
    70 min

    Students evaluate community-scale cooling solutions such as green living roofs, plant gardens and trees, grassy areas instead of asphalt, shade structures, and clean energy, using evidence from their investigations.

    Design
  • 12
    How can we create a cooler community?
    70 min

    As climate champions, students collaboratively design a community map showing solutions to cool their local area. Groups use evidence to design and present their proposals.

    Final Assessment

Final Assessment

UGC Model + Written Explanation

Students develop and revise an argument and model that demonstrate how Earth systems components affect a community's land surface temperature differently, based on evidence of human impacts on land and vegetation. Using the UGC concept icons and Earth Scene, students revisit and revise their earlier model to create a final written explanation of the unit driving question.
