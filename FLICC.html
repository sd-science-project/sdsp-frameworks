Interactive Tool

Science Media Literacy Framework

Grounded in the research of Douglas Allchin (Allchin, 2012; 2022), this interactive framework helps teachers understand how science works as a knowledge-building process and how scientific claims are communicated, translated, and sometimes distorted as they move through media into public discourse. Teachers can also use the framework as a planning tool to identify where students are most likely to encounter science claims and design lessons accordingly.

Science Media Literacy Framework: From Test Tubes to YouTube

Explore the interactive pathway of scientific knowledge from the expert science community into public discourse. This tool maps 29 strategies across the domains of expert science and science communication, helping teachers see how legitimate and bogus claims each travel through distinct channels on their way to students.

Evaluating a Science Claim

An interactive decision-tree tool based on the Fast and Frugal Heuristic for evaluating science claims. This tool gives students a practical, step-by-step approach to quickly assess the credibility of science-related information they encounter online and in the media.

Science Claims in Media Artifacts

A curated collection of real-world science claims from social media, news outlets, and other public sources. Teachers can browse and select science claim phenomena for their science media literacy lessons from this shared library of examples.

LIARS Game

A classroom game developed by Douglas Allchin (2024) where students practice applying the LIARS framework to real science headlines. Students work in teams to determine whether headlines are Legitimate, Imposter, Altered, Recontextualized, or Satirical, building critical evaluation skills through play.

FLICC Taxonomy Tool

An interactive exploration of the 34 techniques of science denial organized across the five FLICC categories: Fake Experts, Logical Fallacies, Impossible Expectations, Cherry Picking, and Conspiracy Theories. Based on the FLICC framework developed by John Cook (Cook, 2020) and the Denial101x course from the University of Queensland and Skeptical Science. Each technique includes a climate example, tips for spotting it, and classroom connections.

Climate Education

Climate Science and Climate Denial

These tools help teachers and students understand how climate science denial and delay tactics work, drawing on the FLICC framework (Cook, 2020) and research on discourses of climate delay (Lamb et al., 2020). Use these resources to support instruction aligned with California's AB285 climate education requirements.

Understanding Climate Denial Tool

Analyze real-world examples of climate denial and delay as they appear in public discourse. This tool supports teachers and students in identifying and categorizing the rhetorical strategies used to undermine confidence in climate science and delay action.

Discourses of Climate Delay

The original research article by Lamb et al. (2020) identifying 12 discourses used to justify inaction on climate change, organized into four strategies: redirect responsibility, push non-transformative solutions, emphasize downsides, and surrender.

Explore Further

Curated external resources for deepening your understanding of science media literacy, climate denial, and the nature of science.

Tools and Courses

  • Denial101x: Making Sense of Climate Science Denial

    Free online course from the University of Queensland that uses the FLICC framework to examine the most common myths about climate change and the techniques used to distort climate science.

  • Skeptical Science

    A comprehensive resource that examines the science behind climate change and addresses the most common arguments used against climate science, organized by scientific topic.

  • Teaching Science Media Literacy (SHiPS Education)

    Douglas Allchin's teacher-facing resources on science media literacy, including the conceptual overview and classroom activities for teaching students to evaluate science claims.

  • Cranky Uncle Game

    John Cook's inoculation-based game where players learn to identify science denial techniques by becoming a cranky uncle. Available as an app for classroom use.


Research Foundations

  • Allchin, D. (2023). Ten competencies for the science misinformation crisis.

    Science Education, 107, 261-274. The foundational framework for the ten competencies that guide this workshop's approach to science media literacy.

  • Lamb, W.F. et al. (2020). Discourses of climate delay.

    Global Sustainability, 3, e17. Identifies 12 discourses that justify inaction on climate change, organized into four strategies: redirect responsibility, push non-transformative solutions, emphasize downsides, and surrender.

  • Cook, J. (2020). A history of FLICC: The 5 techniques of science denial.

    The origin and evolution of the FLICC taxonomy from its roots in Hoofnagle's work on denialism through the development of the Denial101x course and Cranky Uncle game.


California Policy Context

  • California AB285: Climate Change Education

    California law requiring the inclusion of climate change science in the state's Next Generation Science Standards curriculum framework, supporting instruction on climate science across grade levels.

  • NGSS Appendix H: The Nature of Science

    The foundational document connecting Nature of Science understandings to NGSS performance expectations, providing the standards context for science media literacy instruction.
