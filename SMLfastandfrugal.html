Science Media Literacy

Evaluating a Science Claim

An interactive companion to the student handout. Five steps built on the Fast and Frugal decision tree for evaluating science claims, with LIARS and lateral reading as strategies for investigating credibility.

Fast & Frugal (Allchin, 2023; Osborne & Pimentel, 2022) · LIARS (Allchin, 2022) · Lateral Reading (Wineburg & McGrew, 2019)
🛑
Step 1

STOP

Pause before reacting, sharing, or diving in.
▼
When you encounter a science claim in the media, resist the urge to immediately react, share, or form an opinion. Ask yourself: Do I even know what I am looking at? Who made this and why? Am I having an emotional reaction (excitement, anger, fear)? That is exactly when I need to slow down.
This is not a sign of ignorance. Professional fact-checkers do this reflexively. Emotions like fear, anger, or excitement can interfere with your ability to make good judgments. Noticing your emotional state is the first step in thinking clearly.
🧭
Step 2

Take Your Bearings

Now that you have paused, look at the bigger picture before diving in.
▼
Before evaluating the claim, identify what you are looking at. Write the specific claim in your own words, note where you found it and how recent the information is, and identify who made the claim and why. Writing the claim in your own words forces you to process it carefully rather than accepting it at face value.
📝

What is the science claim? Write it in your own words.

📍

Where did you find it and how recent is the information? URL, publication, social media, date.

🔎

Who made this and why? Person, organization, channel. What might their purpose be?

🔒
Step 3 · Gate 1

Is the Source Credible?

Check credibility using LIARS as a first pass, then lateral reading to dig deeper.
▼
This is the first gate of the decision tree. Before you can trust a science claim, you need to know whether the source is credible. Start with LIARS to check for warning signs, then use lateral reading to investigate further.
First Pass: Check for LIARS Tactics
LIARS (Allchin, 2022) identifies five tactics used by imitators of science. If a source uses these tactics, it is performing credibility rather than demonstrating it.
L

Looks

Professional appearance alone is not evidence of credibility.

I

Identity

Appeals to group identity rather than evidence.

A

Acting

Fake or irrelevant credentials used to borrow authority.

R

Repetition

Repeating claims until they seem true.

S

Skepticism

Manufacturing doubt about well-established science.

Dig Deeper: Lateral Reading
Do NOT stay on the source's own website. Open a new browser tab and search for what independent sources say about this source. This is called lateral reading, and it is the single most effective strategy used by professional fact-checkers. Check what Wikipedia, established news outlets, and fact-checking organizations say.

Step 4 · Gate 2

Is There Relevant Expertise?

Does the source have credentials or experience in this specific field?
▼
This is the second gate. A source may be credible in general, but do they have expertise in the relevant field? Not just any field, but the one the claim is about. A nuclear physicist is not an expert on vaccines. A biologist is not an authority on climate modeling.
🎓

📚

Do they have a history of quality work? Recognition by peers? Awards?

🔬

🚫 Fail → Not Trustworthy
🤝
Step 5 · Gate 3

What Is the Consensus?

Do the relevant experts agree? What uncertainty remains?
▼
This is the third and final gate. Even a credible expert can be wrong if they are an outlier. The benchmark for trusting a science claim is whether the community of relevant experts agrees. No single individual can authoritatively claim that they alone are right. Search for what major scientific institutions and organizations say about this topic.
🏛️

Do the relevant experts agree with this claim?

🔍

What do major scientific institutions (NIH, CDC, NASA, IPCC, NAS) say?

⚖️

What uncertainty remains? Is this settled science or an active area of research?

🔄

Is this a single study or finding, or has it been replicated?

✅ Pass → Trust
🚫 Fail → Not Trustworthy
✍️
Verdict

Your Verdict

State your conclusion and explain your reasoning.
▼
Based on your full analysis, choose your verdict: TRUST, PROCEED WITH CAUTION, or DO NOT TRUST. Then explain your reasoning in 2 to 3 sentences. What was the most important piece of evidence that led to your verdict?
👍

TRUST

All three gates passed. The claim is supported by a credible, expert source aligned with scientific consensus.

⚠️

PROCEED WITH CAUTION

Uncertain on one or more gates. Dig deeper before accepting or rejecting the claim.

👎

DO NOT TRUST

Failed one or more gates. The claim is not supported by credible, expert sources aligned with consensus.
