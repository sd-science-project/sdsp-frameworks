Science Media Literacy

Evaluating a Science Claim

An interactive companion to the student handout. Eight steps that combine the SIFT method, the CRAP Test, and the Fast and Frugal Heuristic into one coherent system for evaluating science claims.

SIFT (Caulfield & Wineburg, 2024) · CRAP Test (HHMI BioInteractive / CSU Chico) · Fast & Frugal (Osborne & Pimentel, 2022)
🛑
Step 1

STOP

Pause before reacting, sharing, or diving in.
▼
When you encounter a science claim in the media, resist the urge to immediately react, share, or form an opinion. Ask yourself: Do I even know what I am looking at? Who made this? Am I having an emotional reaction (excitement, anger, fear)? That is exactly when I need to slow down. This is not a sign of ignorance. Professional fact-checkers do this reflexively.
🔍
Step 2

Identify the Claim

Look at the bigger picture before diving in.
▼
Now that you have paused, identify exactly what is being claimed, where you found it, and who is making the claim. Writing the claim in your own words forces you to process it carefully rather than accepting it at face value.
📋
Step 3

First Pass: CRAP Test

A structured first look at the source using four criteria.
▼
The CRAP Test gives you a structured checklist for your initial evaluation of the source. For each criterion, circle Yes or No and write your evidence. This is a starting point, not a final answer. The CRAP Test asks the right questions, but Steps 4 through 6 give you the methods to answer them more rigorously.
C

Currency

Is the information timely and up to date?

R

Reliability

Is the information supported by evidence?

A

Authority

Is the source trustworthy and qualified?

P

Purpose

Is the goal to inform, or to sell/persuade?

↔️
Step 4

Investigate the Source

Open a new tab. Search for what independent sources say.
▼
Do NOT stay on the source's own website. This is called lateral reading. It is the single most effective strategy used by professional fact-checkers. Open a new browser tab and search for the name of the person, organization, or publication making the claim. Check what Wikipedia, established news outlets, and fact-checking organizations say about the source.

📡
Step 5

Find Better Coverage

Search for other credible sources reporting the same claim.
▼
If this is a significant scientific finding, multiple credible outlets should be reporting it. If only one source makes this claim, that is a red flag. Search for other credible sources reporting the same claim and see whether independent reporting confirms or contradicts what you found.
🔗
Step 6

Trace to the Original

Follow links back to the original research, data, or primary source.
▼
Claims often become distorted as they pass through multiple intermediaries. A news article about a study may exaggerate or misrepresent the study's actual findings. Follow links back to the original research or data. Does the original source actually say what the claim says it does?
⚡

Steps 1 through 6 Gathered Your Evidence

Now use that evidence to answer three science-specific questions. You do not need to be an expert to evaluate a scientific claim. Instead of asking "Is this claim true?", ask: "Is this source credible, expert, and aligned with consensus?"

⚖️
Step 7

The Fast and Frugal Decision Tree

Three science-specific questions. If the answer is No to any, the claim is not fully trustworthy.
▼
Use everything you gathered in Steps 1 through 6 to answer these three sequential questions. Each question is a gate: if the answer is No at any point, the claim is not fully trustworthy. You must pass all three to reach a trust verdict.
Question 1
Is the source credible?
Is the source free of conflict of interest? Do they have a track record of honesty? Are they objective and unbiased? (Use what you found in Steps 4 through 6.)
✅ Pass → Continue
🚫 Fail → Not Trustworthy
If Pass, proceed ↓
Question 2
Does the source have relevant expertise?
Does the source have credentials or experience in this specific field? Not just any field, but the relevant one. A nuclear physicist is not an expert on vaccines.
✅ Pass → Continue
🚫 Fail → Not Trustworthy
If Pass, proceed ↓
Question 3
What is the expert consensus?
Do the relevant experts agree with this claim? What do major scientific institutions (NIH, CDC, NASA, IPCC) say? (Use what you found in Step 5.)
✅ Pass → Trust
🚫 Fail → Not Trustworthy
👍

TRUST

All three questions passed. The claim is supported by a credible, expert source aligned with scientific consensus.

⚠️

PROCEED WITH CAUTION

Uncertain on one or more questions. Dig deeper before accepting or rejecting the claim.

👎

DO NOT TRUST

Failed one or more questions. The claim is not supported by credible, expert sources aligned with consensus.

✍️
Step 8

Your Verdict

State your conclusion and explain your reasoning.
▼
Based on your full analysis, choose your verdict: TRUST, PROCEED WITH CAUTION, or DO NOT TRUST. Then explain your reasoning in 2 to 3 sentences. What was the most important piece of evidence that led to your verdict?
